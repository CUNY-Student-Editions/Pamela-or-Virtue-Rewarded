What art thou about to do … God can touch his Heart in an Instant: Pamela’s reflections here bear similarities with a speech against suicide by Philoclea, the sister of Pamela in Sidney’s Arcadia, from which work (with Pope’s ‘Epistle to Miss Blount, with the Works of Voiture’) SR may have taken his heroine’s name. (See Pamela’s continuation of 1741 for explicit play on the coincidence of names (iii. 141; iv. 116; iv. 165); also, for other aspects of the relationship, G. Beer, ‘Pamela: Rethinking Arcadia’, in M. A. Doody and P. Sabor (eds.), Samuel Richardson: Tercentenary Essays (1989), 23–39.) Among Philoclea’s arguments is the point that one should never lose faith in the continuing possibility of divine interposition, ‘since it is not for us to appoint that mighty majesty what time he will help us. The uttermost instant is scope enough for him to revoke everything to one’s own desire’ (The Countess of Pembroke’s Arcadia (The Old Arcadia), ed. J. Robertson (1973), 297). This passage (from bk. iv of the Old Arcadia) was included in published editions of the Arcadia from 1593 onwards, including the version in the fourteenth edition of Sidney’s Works (1724–5), which SR partly printed (Sale, 204).
