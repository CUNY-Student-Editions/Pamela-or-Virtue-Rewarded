Parliament of Women: usually a satirical collocation in the period, as in Charles Johnson, The Wife’s Relief (1712), Epilogue (‘Cou’d we a Parliament of Women call, | We’d vote such Statutes as shou’d Tame you all’); cf. also Nathaniel Lee, The Princess of Cleve (1689), iv.ii; Henry Fielding, The Modern Husband (1732), iv.iii. SR and the women advisers with whom he surrounded himself in the 1750s sometimes called their circle ‘the female senate’ (see e.g. Barbauld, vi. 158 (c. March 1752)).
