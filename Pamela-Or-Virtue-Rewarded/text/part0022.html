GLOSSARY

This glossary defines unfamiliar words and phrases occurring in Pamela, together with unusual or obsolete senses (but not, of course, the only senses) in which familiar words are sometimes used. Richardson was notorious for his lexical freedoms, and in 1754 one contemporary claimed that ‘an odd affected expression’ to be observed throughout his novels was corrupting the language: ‘his many new-coin’d words and phrases … may possibly become current in common Conversation, be imitated by other writers, or by the laborious industry of some future compiler, transferred into a Dictionary’ (Critical Remarks on Sir Charles Grandison, Clarissa and Pamela, 4). Richardson was indeed to become the most frequently cited living author in Samuel Johnson’s Dictionary of the English Language (1755), and it is a measure of his innovative style in Pamela (and in particular of the relative novelty in polite print of a voice in which demotic elements were audible without being mocked) that in OED2 the novel is used to illustrate a high proportion of the rare, obsolete, or vulgar usages listed below. In several other cases, examples from Pamela are absent only because the nineteenth-century edition used in the original OED derived from a revised text in which Richardson had responded to criticism by excising obtrusive instances of slang and dialect from his narrator’s voice, and by making her writing less transgressive of polite registers and linguistic decorums than it originally is.
